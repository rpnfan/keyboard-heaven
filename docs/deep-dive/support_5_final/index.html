From QWERTY to END: The Author’s 1-Year Keyboard Journey#

Article Context Reference:

  • Position: Support 5 of 16 articles
  • Steps covered: 1–7 (complete journey narrative)
  • Purpose: Personal narrative across all steps; motivation from AltGr problem and home-row comfort; realistic timelines; honest trade-off assessment
  • Related articles: Foundation 1-2 (context), Compatible 1 (Spacemak), Decision 1 (Step 6), Custom 1 (anymak:END)
  • Tone: Personal, reflective; problem-driven (AltGr/home-row), optimizing for comfort and efficiency

A Problem Worth Solving#

I started using Vim around the mid-90s. That experience taught me something important: keeping your hands on the home row is powerful. Moving your hands away from the base position breaks rhythm and slows you down. When I could navigate and edit without leaving home row in Vim, everything flowed better.

Years later, when I worked in a lab using German QWERTZ, I hit a daily frustration: symbols needed for LaTeX, programming, and HTML were trapped behind the AltGr layer—backslashes, brackets, curly braces, the @ symbol. AltGr isn’t reachable from home row without uncomfortable hand contortions. And it’s unreliable across different keyboards and systems.

I realized I could fix this. The CapsLock key sat unused. What if I turned it into a layer modifier? Then I could access all those symbols from home row, plus bring navigation keys (arrows, Home, End, PageUp/Down) into reach.

2007–2022: DeutschlandPlus#

I created DeutschlandPlus and used it for 15 years. It was simple: hold CapsLock, and the right hand keys became symbols and navigation. HJKL became arrow keys. Brackets, braces, backslashes—all accessible without moving my hands.

It worked. The layer was transparent to everyone else; I could run it on any lab computer without anyone noticing. Full compatibility with QWERTZ. No relearning needed.

But by 2022, after 15 years, I started wondering: Could I do better?

The answer was yes, but it required something I’d avoided: changing the base layout itself.

2022: Exploring Alternatives#

I tried a custom QWERTZ-like layout (similar to Colemak but for German) and learned it in a month. But by 50 WPM, I quit. The right hand was too busy—the same problem Colemak had. It wasn’t the answer.

Around the same time, I discovered Andreas Wettstein’s ‘opt’ optimizer and his work on KOY (based on AdNW). I realized I could build a layout optimized specifically for English, German, and Dutch—the languages I actually typed.

I spent the rest of 2022 developing a fully optimized layout. Using the ‘opt’ tool extensively, testing practically, tweaking manually. The result: END (English, Deutsch, Nederlands).

2023: Learning Anymak + END#

In 2023, I refined the END layout with ‘opt’ and practical testing. I also realized my layer concept needed updating. Instead of CapsLock, I switched to SpaceFN—using Space as a modifier, which felt more natural. I integrated navigation keys, Enter, and Backspace fully into the layer concept.

This became Anymak: the refined layer system (Steps 1–6) paired with the END alphanumeric layout (Step 7).

Before the switch, I practiced on keybr.com—about 20 minutes most days—until I could type around 40 WPM on the new layout. Then I switched for real.

The learning was intense at first. I reached 60 WPM around month 12. True fluency—where typing felt as automatic and natural as QWERTZ had—took about 2 years. The umlauts were the last thing to feel natural, which was surprising given they were optimized for my actual typing patterns.

After the initial keybr.com practice, I didn’t practice intensively during those 2 years of actual use, which likely extended the fluency timeline. But it also showed that anymak:END works intuitively; you don’t need obsessive drilling for it to become natural.

2024: Finalized#

By 2024, I settled on the complete system with bottom-row modifiers and found hardware I’m satisfied with (a Franken-Lily58 with a 1.75u wide main thumb key).

I’m not tweaking anymore. The system is optimized. The problem I set out to solve—symbols trapped behind AltGr, navigation unreachable from home row, and later, multi-language typing efficiency—is solved.

The Real Trade-Off#

What I gained: symbols and navigation comfortable from home row. Multi-language support without compromise. The satisfaction of solving a 25-year question.

What I lost: flexibility. I can’t walk into an office and use a shared keyboard. Work machines require vanilla QWERTZ. Typing it now feels clumsy.

The cost was real. But so was the benefit.

For Your Own Journey#

Not everyone needs to climb all the way to END. Many people should stop at DeutschlandPlus (15-year sweet spot for good reasons). If you control your devices and have time, Anymak is the practical middle ground.

But if your problem is similar to mine—symbols trapped behind an awkward AltGr layer, or the need for multi-language typing support—you’ll understand why I pursued this far.

Your keyboard is something you touch thousands of times per day. If there’s a problem worth solving, it’s worth solving well.
